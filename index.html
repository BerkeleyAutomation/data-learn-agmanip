Overview

Agriculture remains one of the most important research areas in today's world as we seek to feed an ever-growing population, while dealing with limited water and land, by automating and optimizing inputs to maximize desired yields.

Compared with automation in industrial settings, where objects, poses, and environmental conditions are largely fixed and predetermined, agricultural robotics inherently operates under substantial variability. Tasks such as irrigation, pruning, weeding, and harvesting naturally fit the notion of “Variational Automation” — even among plants of the same species, significant variation arises in geometry, pose, size, maturity, and spatial arrangement due to differences in growth stage, environmental conditions, and biological processes. Outdoor environments add further sources of uncertainty, including changing illumination, weather, occlusions, and terrain conditions. As a result, agricultural robots must continuously adapt their perception, planning, and manipulation strategies to handle diverse task instances rather than repeatedly executing a single predefined workflow.

This fundamental variability makes agriculture an ideal domain for studying variational automation, where robustness, adaptation, and generalization are essential for reliable deployment at scale. In the age of generative AI and foundation models, this workshop brings together researchers, students, industry stakeholders, and practitioners to re-examine these challenges in light of these emerging paradigms.

Topics of Interest

We solicit submissions and discussion on questions including, but not limited to:

  • Multimodal sensing for dexterous plant manipulation
  • Robot navigation in agricultural fields, including orchards, vineyards, and greenhouses
  • Mobile manipulation in agricultural fields, including orchards, vineyards, and greenhouses
  • Digital twins at a variety of layers (2D, 3D, and 4D) for plant growth modeling
  • World models with a variety of input modalities for growth and action prediction
  • Acquiring large-scale simulation data for improved plant modeling
  • Acquiring large-scale real-world plant data for improved plant modeling
  • Using foundation models such as SAM 3 and SAM 3D to improve perception and modeling of plants
  • Obtaining large-scale manipulation data for plants across a variety of robot actions
  • Using LLMs and VLMs to improve agricultural robot policies
  • Approaches for generalization in robot plant manipulation across tasks

Call for Papers

We invite contributions from agriculture researchers applying learning-based methods aligned with any of the topics above. Submissions should be up to 3 pages (excluding references) and will be treated as non-archival.

Papers will be selected based on their relevance to the workshop themes and their potential to seed meaningful discussion among attendees. In line with CoRL 2026 policy, we will not accept papers that have already been published at, or accepted to, the main CoRL 2026 conference. Speakers and participants may, however, highlight relevant agriculture robotics papers appearing at CoRL 2026.

Accepted papers will be spotlighted at the workshop, followed by a poster session, and will be made available on this website. Authors are encouraged to participate in the group discussions.
